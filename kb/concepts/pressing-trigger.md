# Pressing Trigger

> Good pressing is not constant sprinting. It is coordinated aggression activated by the right cue.

## Definition

A pressing trigger is a recognizable moment that signals pressure should intensify. The cue can come from the opponent's body shape, pass direction, touch quality, spacing, or support structure.

The point of a trigger is to turn pressing from effort into structure.

## Common Triggers

- backward pass
- poor first touch
- bouncing or aerial receipt
- receiver facing their own goal
- isolated full-back near the touchline
- slow switch that hangs in the air
- pass into a marked pivot

## What Triggers Do

Triggers allow the pressing team to:

- jump together instead of one by one
- trap the ball in a chosen zone
- protect central space while attacking the receiver
- create turnovers in moments of opponent instability

## Why They Matter

Without triggers, pressing becomes reactive and inconsistent. Some players jump while others hold, which creates holes.

With clear triggers, the team can compress space decisively and with less wasted running.

## Role Implications

Pressing-trigger execution depends on:

- forwards who angle pressure intelligently
- midfielders who recognize the next pass
- defenders willing to hold a high line behind the press
- communicators who synchronize the jump

## Scouting Relevance

When evaluating a presser, look beyond raw intensity.

Useful questions:

- do they jump on the right cue?
- do they shape the press toward help?
- do they recognize when not to jump?
- do they protect the center while pressing outward?

This is a better separator than tackle count or generic work rate.

## Common Failure Modes

- jumping on every pass
- misreading the cue and opening the lane behind
- confusing an individual duel with a team trap
- pressing late, after the receiver has already stabilized

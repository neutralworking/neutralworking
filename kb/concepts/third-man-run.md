# Third-Man Run

> The third man matters because the obvious pass is often the trap. The free player is usually the one arriving after the first exchange.

## Definition

A third-man run happens when two players engage the defense and a third player exploits the space or passing lane that their interaction creates. The point is to move beyond the immediate duel and use timing to access the next action.

This is one of the basic mechanisms of high-level combination play. It helps teams evade pressure without relying on isolated dribbling or forced direct passes.

## What It Is For

- bypass a pressing player
- break lines through combinations
- create forward momentum without risky first-time vertical passes
- free runners between or beyond defenders
- improve chance creation around the box

## Common Patterns

### Wall Pass Into Runner

Player A passes into Player B, who sets the ball into the path of Player C arriving beyond the pressure.

### Bounce to Release

The first receiver does not turn. They only connect the ball quickly so the real progression happens through the third man.

### Blind-Side Arrival

The third player times the run from outside the defender's vision, often into the [[half-space]] Half-Space.

## Why It Works

The defense often focuses on the first receiver and the immediate duel. That attention creates a brief opening for the player who was not the initial target.

Third-man actions are especially effective when:

- the ball moves quickly
- supporting distances are short
- body orientation is clean
- the third player arrives, not waits

## Role Implications

Third-man actions are central to:

- controllers who bounce play cleanly
- attacking eights who arrive into pockets
- false nines connecting under pressure
- wide players making inside runs after a set

## Scouting Relevance

Players who excel in third-man structures usually show:

- quick recognition of combination windows
- clean layoff technique
- timing rather than random movement
- awareness of who the real receiver should be

This is a useful separator between players who combine tidily and players who genuinely accelerate team play.

## Common Failure Modes

- the third player runs too early and crowds the action
- the second player holds the ball too long
- the structure has no spacing behind the combination
- the receiving angle is flat instead of forward-facing

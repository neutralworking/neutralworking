# Field Tilt

> Field tilt is less about who has the ball and more about where the game is being played.

## Definition

Field tilt is a territorial-control concept, often used analytically to describe which team is spending more time, passing volume, or pressure in advanced zones, especially the attacking half or final third.

It is often used as a proxy for territorial dominance rather than pure possession dominance.

## What It Is For

- describe territorial pressure
- separate harmless possession from dangerous occupation
- compare how often teams sustain attacks
- contextualize pressing and rest-defense demands

## Why It Matters

A team can dominate possession without tilting the field. It may circulate safely in harmless zones. Another team may have less possession but spend more of it in threatening territory.

Field tilt helps explain:

- sustained pressure
- territorial compression
- repeated attacks against a set block
- how much defensive stress one team is imposing

## Tactical Implications

High field tilt usually correlates with:

- strong [Rest Defense](/Users/solid-snake/Documents/chief-scout/kb/concepts/rest-defense.md)
- aggressive [Gegenpressing](/Users/solid-snake/Documents/chief-scout/kb/concepts/gegenpressing.md)
- good weak-side occupation
- sustained box-entry pressure

But it does not automatically mean quality chance creation.

## Scouting Relevance

Field tilt is useful context when evaluating players:

- some defenders face constant territorial stress in weak teams
- some midfielders post inflated territorial numbers in dominant systems
- wide players may receive more advanced touches because the field is already tilted in their favor

It is a context metric, not a standalone judgment.

## Common Failure Modes

- treating field tilt as proof of superiority by itself
- ignoring chance quality and box access
- using team territorial dominance to overrate individual control

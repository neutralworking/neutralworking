Pinning

Pinning is how attackers hold defenders in place without always touching the ball.

Definition

Pinning happens when an attacker's position, movement threat, or body orientation prevents a defender from leaving them freely. The defender stays attached because stepping away would expose space behind, beside, or beyond them.

Pinning is one of the quiet mechanisms that make good attacking structures work. It explains why some teams create clean interior spaces even when the ball is nowhere near the pinned player.

What It Is For

  • stop defenders stepping into midfield
  • preserve space between lines
  • create cleaner passing lanes
  • open cut-back zones and interior pockets
  • support overloads elsewhere

How It Works

Last-Line Pinning

Forwards threaten depth so center-backs cannot jump out confidently.

Full-Back Pinning

Wide players hold width and depth so the opposition full-back cannot tuck in early.

Interior Pinning

An attacking midfielder occupies the attention of a pivot or center-back, helping another player receive free.

Pinning is often positional rather than dramatic. The player may barely touch the ball, but they are still shaping the defensive line.

Role Implications

Good pinning is common in:

  • strikers who threaten runs in behind
  • wingers who hold width at the right height
  • attacking midfielders who time occupation of blind spots
  • advanced eights who arrive without collapsing space

It is especially valuable in teams built on overload Overload and half-space Half-Space occupation.

Scouting Relevance

Many attackers are judged only by touches and actions. Pinning reminds scouts to look at what a player's presence does to the line.

Useful questions:

  • do defenders stay deeper because of this player?
  • do they preserve width or depth at the right moment?
  • do they understand when to occupy rather than receive?
  • do their movements free teammates even without direct involvement?

Common Failure Modes

  • standing in the lane without threatening depth
  • drifting toward the ball and freeing defenders to step out
  • occupying the same space as teammates
  • pinning one defender while helping another

Poor pinning clogs structure. Good pinning creates invisible room.

Blind-Side Run

The run works because the defender is looking at the ball and losing sight of the runner.

Definition

A blind-side run is an off-ball movement made behind, across, or just outside the defender's field of vision, usually attacking the shoulder furthest from the ball. The goal is to arrive in the space before the defender can adjust their body and react.

What It Is For

  • create separation without a pure foot race
  • attack gaps between defenders
  • receive in the box with a step advantage
  • exploit defenders focused on the ball

Why It Matters

Many elite attacking movements are not about speed alone. They are about timing against the defender's body orientation. Blind-side runs are one of the clearest examples of movement intelligence creating advantage.

Common Uses

Back-Shoulder Striker Run

The forward attacks the far shoulder of the center-back just as the pass is prepared.

Winger Arriving Inside

The far-side attacker arrives from outside the defender's eyeline into the box.

Midfielder Beyond the Line

An advanced midfielder makes a delayed blind-side run after the defense has focused on the first receiver.

Role Implications

Blind-side movement is particularly valuable for:

  • strikers
  • goal-scoring wide forwards
  • attacking midfielders arriving late

It is often more predictive of dangerous attacking output than raw sprint speed.

Scouting Relevance

Useful questions:

  • does the player time runs against the defender's vision and body shape?
  • do they attack the right shoulder?
  • are their movements coordinated with the passer's rhythm?
  • do they create separation before the pass, not after it?

Common Failure Modes

  • running too early and being tracked easily
  • attacking the visible side of the defender
  • making the run before the passer can execute
  • relying on speed instead of disguise and timing

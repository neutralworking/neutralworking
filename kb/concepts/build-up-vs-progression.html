Build-Up vs Progression

Teams often talk about moving the ball well, but the first problem and the next problem are not the same problem.

Definition

Build-up is the phase where a team escapes or manipulates the first line of pressure. Progression is the phase where the team moves the attack into more dangerous zones after that first problem is solved.

The distinction matters because some players and structures are strong in build-up but weak in progression, while others contribute very little to first-phase control but become decisive once the ball reaches midfield or the half-spaces.

Build-Up

Build-up is mostly about:

  • first-line exits
  • body orientation under pressure
  • goalkeeper and center-back involvement
  • access to the pivot
  • stability behind the ball

Its main question is: can the team start the possession cleanly?

Progression

Progression is mostly about:

  • breaking lines
  • moving into advanced territory
  • receiving between lines
  • carrying or passing through midfield pressure
  • turning possession into territorial or chance value

Its main question is: can the team move the attack into dangerous space?

Why The Distinction Matters

A team can build up well and still progress badly. It may circulate safely at the back but never threaten the next line.

A team can also progress well without elaborate build-up. Direct teams often skip first-phase control and progress by attacking space immediately.

Separating the two phases makes role evaluation sharper.

Role Implications

Strong build-up profiles often include:

  • goalkeeper distributors
  • composed center-backs
  • pivots who offer angles under pressure

Strong progression profiles often include:

  • controllers who break lines
  • eights who receive beyond midfield
  • wide players or forwards who attack advanced space

Some elite players do both. Many do not.

Scouting Relevance

This distinction helps prevent fuzzy evaluation language.

Instead of saying a player is "good in possession," the better questions are:

  • do they solve first-phase pressure?
  • do they move the team forward meaningfully afterward?
  • are they safe only, or genuinely advancing?

Common Failure Modes

  • calling every safe first pass progression
  • mistaking territory for control
  • praising a build-up player for traits they only show once pressure is gone
  • blaming a progression player for structural build-up problems

## Snapshot

![[Pasted image 20240130154224.png]]

Defined by his playing style, leadership qualities, and discipline, Figueroa set the standard for defenders in South America simply by being ahead of his time. First and foremost, the Chilean was a defender who liked defending proactively by anticipating his opponent’s movements in the developing play and jumping in with impeccable timing to make an interception. Comfortable on the ball, Elias Figueroa would often be a surprise threat in the final third of the pitch with his combination play and marauding inside runs, as evidenced by his numbers from his Internacional days, when he scored 27 goals in over 300 appearances.

A combative yet clean dueler, Figueroa would use his physicality to his advantage on the ground or in the air. His spotless tackling, almost always within the bounds of the game’s rules and regulations, meant he would earn just one red card across a career that stretched two decades. As a leader in defence, the Chilean would captain several of his club sides and eventually the national team, guiding the latter through their first golden age that encompassed three intermittent FIFA World Cup appearances (1966, 1974, and 1982) and a third and a second-place finishes in the Copa America.

Elias Figueroa was an outlier in pure talent, a notion picked up by the Chile national team, who folded him into the senior set-up while he was still in his late teens. He would eventually earn 47 caps for La Roja between 1966 and 1982 and retire with two goals and a place in the 1974 FIFA World Cup All-Star team. His unparalleled skill set was also recognised quickly by some of the continent’s best domestic sides, namely Penarol and Internacional, whom he represented during the best years of his career. Born in Valparaiso, the Jewel of the Pacific on the western coastline of Chile, Figueroa spent his youth and early senior career with Santiago Wanderers. In 1967, the defender joined Uruguayan giants Penarol, still in the nascent stage of his career and immediately settled into a team considered then by many as the best in the world. He won the domestic championship with Penarol in 1967 and 1968 and made over 200 appearances for the club during his four-year spell.

FigueroaIn 1972, Figueroa left Penarola to spend the next five years of his domestic career with Brazil’s Internacional. During a sparkling period in the club’s history, Figueroa helped Internacional win the state title every year of his tenure and the national championship in 1975 and 1976. Cementing himself as one of the best players in the world, the Chilean also collected the bulk of his individual honours while in Brazil. At the end of his Internacional stint, Elias Figueroa returned to his native Chile to help Palestino win the cup and the league in 1977 and 1978. He spent a year in the United States of America later with Fort Lauderdale Strikers and another back in Chile with Colo-Colo before retiring from the sport in 1982.

# 4-4-1-1

A variation of 4–4–2 with one of the strikers playing "in the hole", or as a "[second striker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_striker "Second striker")", slightly behind their partner.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-renamed_from_4411_on_20140825221137-19) The second striker is generally a more creative player, the playmaker, who can drop into midfield to pick up the ball before running with it or passing to teammates.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-renamed_from_4411_on_20140825221137-19) Interpretations of 4–4–1–1 can be slightly muddled, as some might say that the extent to which one forward has dropped off and separated from the other can be debated.

In the [2015 FIFA Women's World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2015_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup "2015 FIFA Women's World Cup") semi-final match against [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany_women%27s_national_football_team "Germany women's national football team"), [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_women%27s_national_soccer_team "United States women's national soccer team") manager [Jill Ellis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jill_Ellis "Jill Ellis") moved from a traditional 4–4–2 with [Abby Wambach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abby_Wambach "Abby Wambach") and [Alex Morgan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alex_Morgan "Alex Morgan") up top, to a 4–4–1–1 formation replacing Wambach with a withdrawn [Carli Lloyd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carli_Lloyd "Carli Lloyd") in the hole as a playmaker. The Americans, who had underwhelmed to that point in the tournament, controlled the match for a 2–0 win. The defensive play of [Morgan Brian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morgan_Gautrat "Morgan Gautrat") and [Lauren Holiday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lauren_Holiday "Lauren Holiday") was credited by [NBC Sports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NBC_Sports "NBC Sports") as unlocking the formation's attacking potential.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-21) The German team's stronger midfield stretched out of shape to combat the Americans' wide threats, opening space for Lloyd's central distribution.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-22)

In the [2019 FIFA Women's World Cup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_FIFA_Women%27s_World_Cup "2019 FIFA Women's World Cup") semi-finals, [Phil Neville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phil_Neville "Phil Neville") employed the 4–4–1–1 as manager of [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England_women%27s_national_football_team "England women's national football team") against the United States, but deployed right attacker [Nikita Parris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikita_Parris "Nikita Parris") out of position in the hole and forward [Beth Mead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beth_Mead "Beth Mead") also out of position deeper in the midfield. Former Lioness-turned-commentator [Eniola Aluko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eniola_Aluko "Eniola Aluko") said the tactical shift "failed massively" as the United States scored in the 10th minute and again in the first half, while England struggled to connect play. Despite a reversion to a 4–2–3–1 at half, the Lionesses lost 2–1.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-24)

# 3-2-3-2 (WW)

### WW

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Aranycsapat.png/170px-Aranycsapat.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Aranycsapat.png)

The WW formation used by Hungary's [Golden Team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Team "Golden Team").

The WW formation (also known as the MM formation, according to the current diagram convention, that is goalkeeper at the bottom. However, it is called the WW formation if the goalkeeper is depicted at the top as was customary at the time), was a development on the WM formation. It was created by Hungarian [Márton Bukovi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%A1rton_Bukovi "Márton Bukovi"), who turned the 3–2–2–3/WM formation into a 3–2–3–2 by effectively turning the forward "M" upside down (that is M to W).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_(association_football)#cite_note-sebes-9) The lack of an effective centre-forward in Bukovi's team necessitated moving a forward back to midfield to create a playmaker, with another midfielder instructed to focus on defence. This transformed into a 3–2–1–4 formation when attacking and turned back to 3–2–3–2 when possession is lost. This formation has been described by some as somewhat of a genetic link between the WM and 4–2–4 and was also successfully used by Bukovi's compatriot [Gusztáv Sebes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guszt%C3%A1v_Sebes "Gusztáv Sebes") for the [Hungarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungary_national_football_team "Hungary national football team") [Golden Team](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Team "Golden Team") in the early 1950s.
